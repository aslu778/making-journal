For this project, I decided to track each time I saw my own reflection through a mirror, a window, or a shiny surface. I did this by categorising and recording the type of surface, time of day, how I felt in that moment, and what I did afterward. I was drawn to this idea because reflections are everywhere, yet we rarely pay attention to them, and we often have unconscious encounters with ourselves. I wanted to see what would happen if I made those moments visible.

Collecting the data was difficult for me. At first, I was too aware of every reflection I might see. But over time, the act of recording became more natural. Sometimes I thought about the times when my reflection would show, but I might not have realised. Visualising the data through a personal visual language by using colour, shape, size, and texture transformed the experience as it made me notice patterns I wouldn't have otherwise. For example, I most often saw myself in windows during moments like leaving the house, walking past shops, and commuting. When I saw myself in mirrors, it was more through routines like getting ready or ending the day. Shiny surfaces, like my phone screen, caught me off guard as I may not have properly counted all the times my reflection had shown. I also noticed that my emotional response varied not just with mood, but with lighting and context, as sometimes I felt interested or worried about my reflection, other times, just neutral. In designing my data collection, I chose to emphasise surface type, time, emotion, and post-reflection action. I left out shadows, dark reflections, how long I looked at myself (not to the second), and the very smallest reflections. Sometimes during the experiment, I may not have noted the times I saw my reflection, as I was too busy or didn't take notice while working or being in class.

This exercise aligns with data humanism and the Dear Data project, as I wasn't trying to quantify myself but understand myself differently. I tried to design my visuals to feel expressive and emotional. Ultimately, this project made me more aware of how often I encounter myself, and how it shapes my sense of identity.
