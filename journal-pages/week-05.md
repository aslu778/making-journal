[Reflective Proposal](<../assets/week-05/Reflective Proposal.pdf>)


Across my Making Journal, the experiments I enjoyed most were the data drawings and the
interactive sketches. Data drawings felt natural because they were hands-on and let me
think through information visually, rather than forcing it into strict charts. I liked being able to
sketch patterns and rhythms in a way that felt close to the experience itself. The interactive
tasks were also exciting because simple bits of code could suddenly make the data move or
respond. These experiments made me realise that I like data that feels alive, flexible, and
open to interpretation. They also made me think more about the datafication of everyday life,
how ordinary actions can be measured as units, and how much interpretation sits behind
that process. These reflections have shaped the direction of my project and pushed me
toward exploring the hidden structures of daily routines.

For my project, I plan to focus on the datafication of everyday commuting in a near‑future
scenario where “smart mobility optimization” becomes a normal part of city life. In this
imagined world, public transport systems, footpaths, and shared spaces are filled with
sensors that track movement patterns, walking speed, waiting time, and route choices.
These systems are marketed as tools for efficiency and sustainability, promising smoother
travel and reduced congestion. But they also flatten the complexity of how people actually
move through a city and raise questions about who controls the data and the experiences
represented. It links directly to ideas about data sovereignty, especially the idea that
communities should have authority over the data collected about them.

The data for this project will be collected manually by myself through a structured
observation protocol. Over several days, I will track my own commuting routines at regular
intervals, noting actions such as walking, waiting, transferring, pausing, and navigating
crowded spaces. This data will be real and live, but also interpretive, because I am the one
deciding what counts as an action and how to record it. I will then translate this information
into a series of data drawings that visually express the rhythms, interruptions, and flows of
everyday commuting. These drawings will form the foundation for a speculative visualisation
that imagines how a future mobility‑tracking system might interpret, simplify, or distort the
same data.

The proposed data‑driven visualisation will take the form of an interactive interface built from
my data drawings, using them as the base layer rather than replacing them with standard
charts. The interface will mimic the look and tone of a future “smart mobility” dashboard, but
it will exaggerate its logic to make its assumptions visible. As users interact with the system,
they can toggle between my hand‑drawn interpretations of the commuting data and the
automated classifications generated by the speculative dashboard. This contrast is central to
the visualization designed to show how community‑generated, situated knowledge can be
flattened or overwritten when data is processed through systems that prioritize optimization
over lived experience. The intended impact is to prompt viewers to question who benefits
from these systems, who is left out, and what forms of knowledge are lost when data
sovereignty is not respected. Ultimately, the project aims to highlight the value of nuance,
context, and human interpretation in understanding the rhythms of moving through a city.
