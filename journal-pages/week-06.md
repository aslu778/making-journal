## In-class Activity

1. Data Exploration (30 mins)
Data source and origin
The data for this project will be self-generated through a structured observation protocol of my daily commute. Over multiple days, I will manually record my movements at regular intervals, documenting actions such as walking, waiting, transferring between transport modes, pausing, and navigating crowded areas. This data is experiential and situated, reflecting my own embodied interaction with the city rather than an external dataset.
Data contents and structure
The dataset will be organised as a time-based log. Each entry will include:

Timestamp
Location/context (e.g. bus stop, platform, street)
Action type (walking, waiting, transferring, etc.)
Duration
Environmental notes (crowdedness, delays, interruptions)

This will likely be structured as a simple table or spreadsheet, which can then be translated into visual sketches and drawings. The structure is intentionally lightweight and flexible to allow interpretive variation.
Limitations, biases, and gaps
This dataset is inherently subjective, as I am both the observer and recorder. Decisions about what counts as an “action” introduce bias and inconsistency. It also represents only one commuter’s experience, limiting its generalisability. However, these limitations are conceptually important—they highlight how all data collection involves interpretation. Compared to automated systems, this dataset foregrounds nuance and ambiguity, which aligns with the project’s critique of datafication and reinforces the focus on data sovereignty and situated knowledge.

Visual Research and Precedent Study
1. Dear Data (Lupi & Posavec)

I am drawn to its combination of hand-drawn aesthetics and personal data storytelling.
I will carry forward its emphasis on subjective interpretation and visual nuance.
This reinforces my direction toward blending data and drawing rather than standard charts.

2. Transit system dashboards (e.g. smart city interfaces)

I am interested in their clean, authoritative, efficiency-driven aesthetic.
I will adopt visual cues like grids, metrics, and simplified categories.
This reinforces the speculative dashboard concept as a critique.

3. Giorgia Lupi’s data humanism approach

I am drawn to the idea of data as emotional and expressive rather than purely analytical.
I will incorporate variability and imperfection in my drawings.
This strengthens my focus on human interpretation as central.

4. Google Maps / mobility tracking interfaces

I am drawn to their clarity and authority in representing movement.
I will exaggerate features like optimisation paths and time efficiency metrics.
This helps me develop the “institutional”的 visual language of my dashboard.

5. Critical design/speculative UI projects

I am interested in how they use familiar interfaces to expose hidden assumptions.
I will carry forward the use of subtle exaggeration and irony.
This pushes my project further into critique rather than just representation.


3. Project Planning and Skills Roadmap
3.1 What do I need to make?
I will produce:

A set of hand-drawn data visualisations representing my commute rhythms
A digital interactive interface that mimics a speculative smart mobility dashboard
A toggle system allowing users to switch between:

Hand-drawn, interpretive data view
Automated, simplified system interpretation



The interface will visually contrast:

Organic, irregular drawing styles
Clean, rigid, grid-based dashboard aesthetics

Annotations in my diagram include:

User interaction (click/hover toggles)
Data transformation pipeline (raw → interpreted → systemised)
Visual layering between human and machine perspectives


3.2 What do I need to learn? (ranked)

Interactive web design (HTML/CSS/JavaScript or p5.js) – to build the interface
Data structuring and parsing – translating logs into usable formats
Mapping drawing aesthetics into digital form – scanning/vectorising or recreating
UI/UX design principles for dashboards – especially authoritative aesthetics
Basic animation or interaction techniques – toggles, transitions, responsiveness


3.3 What are my next steps? (≈170 words)
My immediate priority is to begin collecting my commuting dataset using the structured observation method I outlined. At the same time, I will define a consistent data format to ensure the information can be translated into both drawings and digital outputs. Alongside this, I will start producing initial hand-drawn visualisations to explore how rhythm, interruption, and movement can be represented visually.
In parallel, I will begin developing my technical skills in interactive design, starting with simple JavaScript or p5.js experiments to test toggling between different visual states. This will help me understand how to build the dual-layer interface central to my concept.
I will also refine the visual language of the speculative dashboard, experimenting with typography, grids, and metric displays that communicate authority while subtly exposing their reductive logic. These steps will allow me to connect my conceptual framework with practical execution and ensure that both the data and the interface evolve together.

## Independent Study
1. Consultation Reflection (≈170 words)
The consultation feedback highlighted the strength of my dual-layer concept, particularly the idea of using methodology itself as a form of critique. A key takeaway was the suggestion to further develop the visual language of the speculative dashboard. This shifted my focus from simply building an interface to carefully designing its aesthetic as an argument. I now see the dashboard not just as a container for data, but as a system that communicates authority through specific visual choices such as grids, typography, and metrics.
The feedback also encouraged me to think about exaggeration as a strategy—pushing optimisation logic further to reveal its assumptions. This has sharpened my direction, making the contrast between hand-drawn and automated data more intentional and legible.
As a result, I will be more deliberate in designing how the interface looks and behaves, ensuring it clearly reflects institutional control while revealing its limitations. This will guide my next steps in both visual experimentation and technical development.

2. Technical Skill Building
Focus: Basic interactive sketches (p5.js / JavaScript)
I started experimenting with simple interactive sketches that respond to user input, such as toggling shapes or switching between visual states. I created a basic prototype where clicking changes between two representations of the same dataset (e.g. organic vs grid-based layout).
What I learned:

How to structure simple interaction logic (e.g. mousePressed events)
How to overlay and switch between visual layers
The importance of keeping data and visuals modular

Outcome:
This experiment directly supports my project by demonstrating how I can build the toggle between hand-drawn and system-generated views. It also revealed that I need to simplify my visuals initially before layering complexity.

3. Initial Concept Sketch
I developed a more refined sketch of the interface, which includes:

A split or toggle-based screen
Left side: hand-drawn commuting data (fluid, irregular, annotated)
Right side: system dashboard (grid-based, labelled, quantified)
Buttons or switches to activate “optimised view” or “raw experience”

I also tested a rough digital layout showing:

Movement lines becoming simplified paths
Waiting time converted into efficiency scores

This early prototype helps visualise how the system might flatten lived experience and confirms the strength of the contrast as the core of my project.
